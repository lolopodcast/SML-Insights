McKinsey's Challenge: When Clients Turn to AI, How Do We Transform?

HBR
Harvard Business Review · Interview by Adi Ignatius
0:00 / 0:00

As McKinsey celebrates its centennial, Global Managing Partner Bob Sternfels discusses how the world's most influential consulting firm is reinventing itself for the AI era—shifting from advisory services to outcomes-based partnerships while rebuilding trust after high-profile scandals.

5 Key Insights

01

From Advisory to Outcomes Model

McKinsey is rapidly shifting from pure advisory work to an outcomes-based model where they underwrite business case results. Currently, about one-third of revenue comes from this approach, with goals to exceed 50% during Sternfels' tenure.

02

Resilience Over Perfect Grades

AI analysis of 20 years of hiring data revealed that candidates who experienced setbacks and recovered are more likely to become partners. McKinsey now actively tests for resilience in environments where no one has pattern recognition.

03

The "25 Squared" Workforce Strategy

McKinsey is growing client-facing roles by 25% while reducing back-office positions by 25%. AI saved 1.5 million hours in search and synthesis work last year, enabling consultants to focus on higher-value problems.

04

CEOs' Top Two Concerns

First: "How do I get value from AI technology?" Second: "How do I build institutional resilience?" Leaders increasingly accept that things will never return to how they were before.

05

Learning from Scandals: Humble Yet Courageous

McKinsey conducted deep soul-searching after the opioid crisis scandal, implementing stricter client selection standards. However, they also learned to push back on criticism when they believe they're right—such as their work with hard-to-abate sectors on climate transition.

Alpha School: A New Approach To Education

α
Alpha School · MacKenzie Price
0:00 / 0:00

Alpha School represents a revolutionary approach to K-12 education, combining AI-powered personalized learning with a mastery-based progression model. Students complete core academics in just 2 hours daily, freeing time for sports, arts, and life skills development.

Part I: Educational Model

01

From Time-Based to Mastery-Based

The core problem with traditional education is the time-based model where students advance by age rather than mastery. This creates cumulative knowledge gaps—a 7th grader failing chemistry may not be struggling with chemistry itself, but with 4th grade fractions they never mastered. Alpha requires 90% mastery before advancing to the next unit, fundamentally breaking the "attendance equals completion" paradigm.

02

Time Freedom as Core Motivation

The biggest effect size comes from giving time back to students. Framing the goal as "2 hours of learning" lets kids have time for sports, arts, and workshops—this motivates more than simply promising "you'll learn 2x faster." This overturns the traditional assumption that "more time = more learning," replacing it with "focused short learning + rich extracurricular development."

03

Guides Replace Teachers

At Alpha School, teachers transform from traditional grading and lesson planning roles into supporting students' emotional and motivational needs while teaching life skills. This shift frees teacher time to mentor, motivate, and coach students into becoming self-directed learners. Subject instruction goes to AI; humans focus on emotional connection and character development that AI cannot replace.

04

Age Grade ≠ Knowledge Grade

Many "A students" enter anywhere from 1 year ahead to 3 years behind; "B students" may be 3 to 7 years behind. Alpha starts with diagnosis and can quickly help them catch up. This reveals the true ability gaps hidden by traditional report cards and explains why many students "suddenly" fall behind—it's actually long-accumulated foundational gaps.

05

High Standards Create Happiness

Contrary to the current trend of lowering expectations to avoid struggle, the key to children's confidence and happiness is achieving high standards through effort and resilience. Alpha's data shows about 96% of students report loving school, proving that rigorous demands and learning enthusiasm are not contradictory.

Part II: AI Applications

06

Precise Diagnosis: Finding the Real Problem

The AI tutor brings students to the exact lessons they need to learn. The system identifies students' true knowledge gaps—a student stuck at 740 on SAT math was diagnosed by Alpha as lacking 3rd grade arithmetic fluency; after reinforcement, they jumped to 790. AI doesn't teach more; it teaches "the right things."

07

Dynamic Adjustment: Zone of Proximal Development

If students start feeling overwhelmed, the system lowers difficulty to bring them back to their zone of proximal development; conversely, fast learners need to progress quickly. If you only need 5 repetitions to understand a concept, you shouldn't be forced to sit through 10; if I need 15 repetitions, I shouldn't only get 10. This is personalized pacing that traditional one-to-many classrooms can never achieve.

08

Real-Time Feedback Loop: Behavioral Intervention

AI analyzes the accuracy and speed of students completing lessons in the background, then provides guidance: "We notice your accuracy is a bit low because when you get answers wrong, you skip to the next question without reading the explanation, so we're going to slow down." AI doesn't just look at "right or wrong"—it observes "learning behavior" and corrects it in real-time.

09

Curriculum Curation: Cross-Platform Best Combinations

Not all apps excel equally at all grade levels or all subjects. Alpha has spent 11 years curating which curricula and apps work best for teaching different subjects—whether math, reading, science, or languages. AI's value isn't in any single tool, but in integrating multiple tools and selecting the best-fit resources for each student.

10

Adaptive Algorithms, Not Generative AI

The so-called "2-hour AI learning" is closer to 3 hours, with a student-to-guide ratio of about 5:1, and no "generative AI" is used. Alpha's AI core is an adaptive system for diagnosis, recommendation, tracking, and feedback—not ChatGPT-style dialogue generation. This reminds us: the value of educational AI lies not in "how advanced" it is, but in "how precisely it solves learning problems."
